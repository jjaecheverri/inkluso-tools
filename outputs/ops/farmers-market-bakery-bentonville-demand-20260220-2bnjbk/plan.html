Farmers Market Bakery
Bentonville, AR — 90-Day Demand Plan

Produced by Juan Camilo Echeverri & Camilo Osorio · IN-KluSo / Xtatik · February 20, 2026 · Revenue range: $5k–$20k/mo · Risk tolerance: Medium
Problem Summary

The gap between great product and reliable revenue

A bakery with farmers market distribution is not yet a business — it is a recurring pop-up. The core problem is not product quality; it is demand architecture.

Farmers market bakeries in the $5k–$20k monthly revenue band share a structural ceiling: revenue is episodic (market days only), audience capture is zero-sum (browsers, not subscribers), and repeat buyers exist but are never systematically converted. The result is a business that is perpetually starting over each week.

Bentonville-specific context: the city has an unusually dense concentration of high-income, food-literate households tied to Walmart HQ, supplier networks, and the Crystal Bridges/Momentary cultural ecosystem. Demand exists. The problem is access architecture, not appetite.

Diagnosis

Mechanism: Single-channel, zero-capture distribution

The current model captures transaction but not relationship. Each market day resets to zero: no pre-committed buyers, no predictable volume, no ability to plan production efficiently. This produces three compounding failures:

  • Production inefficiency: Baking to speculative demand means systematic over or under-production. Both are expensive.
  • Revenue variance: Weather, competing events, and market attendance fluctuations outside your control set your revenue ceiling each week.
  • Zero compounding: Satisfied customers leave without a mechanism to return on a committed schedule. Word-of-mouth exists; repeat purchase architecture does not.

The 90-day intervention target is converting this from a transactional pop-up into a demand-predictable micro-distribution system with at least two revenue streams.

Constraints

What the plan must respect

  • Capital: Medium risk tolerance, no assumed capital injection. Interventions funded from operating cash flow.
  • Production capacity: All interventions must stay within current production ceiling.
  • Regulatory: Arkansas cottage food law allows direct-to-consumer sales with revenue caps; B2B wholesale requires commercial kitchen licensing.
  • Time: 90-day window. No interventions requiring more than 30 days of setup before first revenue signal.
Intervention Tracks

Three parallel moves, sequenced by capital risk

Track 1 — Low Capital

Pre-order subscription: convert browsers into committed buyers

Launch a weekly pre-order system before the next market cycle. Buyers commit by Wednesday for Saturday pickup. Start with 2–3 box configurations (e.g., $28 bread box, $38 pastry mix, $55 full-week box). Target 20 committed orders in the first 30 days.

  • Tools: Google Forms or Square (both free tier sufficient at this scale)
  • Capture emails at market with a physical sign-up sheet, first order discount
  • Send a weekly SMS/email reminder Wednesday with that week's menu
  • Benefit: predictable production volume by Wednesday, zero food waste on committed items
  • First irreversible commitment: printing sign-up materials (~$20)
Track 2 — Structural

B2B micro-channel: one wholesale account in 60 days

Bentonville has a high concentration of boutique coffee shops, coworking spaces, and corporate campus cafeterias that source locally as a brand signal. One B2B account at $400–$800/week represents a 20–40% revenue floor stabilizer.

  • Target: 3–5 outreach conversations in weeks 3–4; close 1 account by day 60
  • Regulatory check required: confirm Arkansas cottage food law revenue cap before outreach
  • Pricing logic: wholesale at 60–65% of retail maintains margin
  • Candidate targets: Onyx Coffee Lab, local coworking spaces, Crystal Bridges member events
Track 3 — Defensive

Cost floor and waste reduction: stabilize before scaling

Eliminate cost leakage before expanding revenue channels. Know your exact break-even at $5k, $10k, and $15k monthly revenue before committing to any Track 2 contracts.

  • Recipe standardization: document cost-per-unit for every item currently sold
  • Waste log: track unsold inventory per market day for 4 weeks
  • Pricing audit: compare to comparable Bentonville market vendors
  • Cash buffer target: 30-day operating expenses in reserve before signing any B2B contract

First Irreversible Step

At your next market day, place a physical sign-up sheet on your table with three pre-order box options and a price for each. Collect 10 email addresses before you pack up. If 10 people sign up, you have validated demand. If fewer than 5 do, the offer needs adjustment before you invest in any infrastructure.

Risks

What this plan does not guarantee

High
Regulatory exposure on B2B wholesale
Arkansas cottage food law restricts wholesale without commercial kitchen certification. Confirm status with the Arkansas Department of Health before signing any wholesale contract.
Medium
Pre-order adoption rate may be slow
Farmers market buyers are habituated to impulse purchase. Plan for 4–6 weeks of friction before subscription behavior normalizes.
Medium
Production capacity ceiling
If pre-orders and B2B demand exceed kitchen capacity simultaneously, fulfillment quality degrades. Set a production cap before launching both tracks.
Low
Seasonal demand fluctuation
Bentonville farmers markets typically run March–November. B2B channels are less seasonal and provide a partial hedge.
